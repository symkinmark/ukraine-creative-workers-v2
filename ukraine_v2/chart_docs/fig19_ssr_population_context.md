# Fig 19 — Annual Death Rate by Group, 1921-1992 (Normalised)

**File:** `charts/fig19_ssr_population_context.png`

## What this chart shows
A time-series line chart showing the **% of each group dying per year** from 1921 to 1992. Each data point = (deaths in group that year) ÷ (total group size) × 100. This normalises for the fact that groups are different sizes — so you're seeing mortality rate, not raw death count.

## Key finding (V2.3)
**The deported group shows a catastrophic mortality spike in 1937-1938.** At its 1937 peak, **35.5% of all deportees died in a single year**. In 1938, another 15.3%. Together 1937-1938 consumed ~51% of the entire deported group. This is not an accident-rate spike — it is organised mass killing.

The other groups show relatively flat lines throughout (0-2% per year = normal background mortality). The deported spike is so severe it dominates the entire chart visually.

**V2.3 deported peak years (n=195 total):**
| Year | Deaths | % of group |
|------|--------|-----------|
| 1937 | 65 | 35.5% |
| 1938 | 28 | 15.3% |
| 1942 | 12 | 6.6% |

## What to look for
- The deported line (dark/purple) spiking to 36.5% in 1937
- All other lines staying at 0-2% per year throughout — the deported group is uniquely catastrophic
- The shaded period labels (Great Terror, WWII) marking which historical events produced which spikes
- After 1945, the deported line returns to near-zero — most deportees already dead

## V2.3 vs V2.1/V2.2
Deported group grew from n=75 (V2.1) → n=178 (V2.2) → n=195 (V2.3). The 1937 spike % is now 35.5% (V2.3) vs 36.5% (V2.2) — because the 5 new V2.3 deported entries died in years other than 1937, diluting the peak slightly. The absolute 1937 deaths (n=65) are unchanged.

## Why normalisation was necessary
Non-migrated has n=6,000 vs deported n=178. A raw count chart would show a huge non-migrated bar simply because there are more of them — making the deported catastrophe invisible. Normalising to % lets you compare fairly.
