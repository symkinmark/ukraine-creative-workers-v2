# Fig 21 — Mean Age at Death: Our Groups vs Soviet Republic General Populations

**File:** `charts/fig21_soviet_republic_comparison.png`

## What this chart shows
External validation chart. Plots our four migration groups' mean age at death by decade of death (period data), overlaid against published life expectancy (e₀) estimates for four Soviet republic groupings: Baltic SSRs, Ukrainian SSR, Russian SFSR, and Central Asian SSRs.

X-axis is death decade (1920s-1980s), clipped at 1990 (Soviet dissolution boundary). Y-axis is mean age at death / life expectancy e₀ (years).

## Key finding (V2.3 — n: migrated 1,280 / non-migrated 6,030 / deported 183)
- **Migrated group** tracks close to or above Baltic SSR general life expectancy (e₀) — the best-performing Soviet republics. By the 1980s, migrants were dying at age 77, above the Baltic general population.
- **Non-migrated group** tracks close to Ukrainian SSR general population life expectancy (e₀) — which is expected and validates the dataset.
- **Deported group** falls below Central Asian SSR general life expectancy (e₀) in early decades (the worst-performing region in the Soviet Union). Their mortality is that severe.

## What to look for
- Where our lines (mean age at death) sit relative to the republic reference lines (e₀) — this is external validation
- The non-migrated group should roughly track the Ukrainian SSR e₀ line (and it does)
- The migrated group's line rising above all Soviet-era reference lines in later decades = diaspora in the West benefiting from Western medicine and living standards
- The Great Terror & Holodomor shading (1930s-40s) — all groups dip there

## Data sources for reference lines
- Meslé & Vallin (2003) — Ukrainian/Russian LE reconstruction pre-1959
- Andreev et al. (1998) — Soviet republic comparisons
- UN WPP 2022 — post-1959 figures
- Katus et al. (2002) — Baltic SSR estimates
