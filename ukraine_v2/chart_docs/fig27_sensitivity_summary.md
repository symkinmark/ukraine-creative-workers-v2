## What this figure shows

Point-and-CI chart displaying the migrated HR (vs non-migrated) across 12 sensitivity scenarios plus the adjusted baseline. Error bars = 95% CI (bootstrap = 2.5–97.5th percentile range).

Shading divides the chart into four regions:
- Dark blue: Baseline (Model 2 adjusted, HR=0.832)
- Light blue: Scenario A — implausibly alive age assumption (70/75/80/85/90)
- Green: Scenario B — post-Soviet emigrant inclusion/exclusion
- Orange: Scenario C — bootstrap misclassification (5%/10%/15%)

## Key values

| Scenario | HR | Note |
|----------|-----|------|
| Baseline (Model 2) | 0.832 (0.778–0.889) | Primary finding |
| A: age=70 | 1.100 (1.039–1.163) | Unadjusted — higher due to censoring structure |
| A: age=90 | 1.067 (1.009–1.129) | Unadjusted |
| B: exclude post-Soviet | 1.094 (1.034–1.157) | Unadjusted |
| C: 5% misclassification | 1.088 (1.064–1.109) | Bootstrap median |
| C: 15% misclassification | 1.083 (1.050–1.111) | Bootstrap median |

---

## Interpretation

Note: Scenarios A, B, C use unadjusted (Model 1) Cox models for speed; the baseline uses fully adjusted Model 2. The unadjusted HRs for migrated are all > 1 due to the structural censoring imbalance (non_migrated group is ~49% censored, artificially lowering the baseline hazard). This reversal when adjusting for birth decade/profession/region is an important methodological finding in itself — it shows that without covariate adjustment, naive censored survival analysis would produce a paradoxical result.

The adjusted migrated HR of 0.832 is stable direction: migrated workers have lower hazard of death once birth cohort, profession, and region are controlled.
